## The Structure of Reality `[CANON — UESP]`

### Aetherius `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Aetherius — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Aetherius

- The realm of **pure magic**; exists beyond Oblivion.
- This is where the **Aedra** went after sacrificing their power to create Mundus; it is the source of all magic in Nirn.
- **The stars** are holes punched through the membrane of Oblivion by **Magnus** (the God of Magic) and the **Magna-Ge** when they fled Creation — their light is raw Aetherius shining through. `[CANON — IN-GAME BOOK: The Monomyth]`
- The sun is the largest "hole" — Magnus's own exit wound. `[CANON — IN-GAME BOOK: The Monomyth]`

### Oblivion `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Oblivion — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Oblivion

- The "void" that surrounds Mundus; home of the **Daedra**.
- Not a single realm — it is an infinite collection of **Planes of Oblivion**, each ruled by a **Daedric Prince** or lesser Daedra.
- Mortals can be pulled into Oblivion or travel there, but it is extremely dangerous without divine protection.

### Mundus `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Mundus — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Mundus

- The "mortal plane" — the creation that the Aedra sacrificed their power to stabilise.
- Contains **Nirn** (the world), **Masser** and **Secunda** (the two moons), and the **Aetherius** above (accessed through Magnus's holes in the sky).
- Mundus is inherently unstable and would dissolve without the **Towers** holding it together. `[CANON — UESP]`
- The Aedra are weakened versions of themselves within Mundus because of the sacrifice they made to create it. `[CANON — UESP]`

### Nirn `[CANON — UESP]`
- The physical world; home of all mortals.
- Contains the continent of **Tamriel** (the known world), plus **Akavir** (east), **Atmora** (north), **Yokuda** (west, mostly sunk), **Pyandonea** (south), and **Aldmeris** (unknown/possibly lost).

## The et'Ada — Original Spirits `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:et'Ada — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Et%27Ada  
> **Source:** In-game book: The Monomyth

- The **et'Ada** ("original spirits" or "ancestor spirits") are the fundamental beings that existed before Mundus.
- They existed in a state of pure potential; the act of **creating Mundus** required them to define themselves (limiting their infinite nature) and sacrifice portions of their power.
- Those who participated in Creation → **Aedra** ("our ancestors"). They became gods but weaker.
- Those who refused → **Daedra** ("not our ancestors"). They retained full power but have no stake in Mundus.
- ⚠️ The distinction Aedra = good / Daedra = evil is **a mortal oversimplification**. The Daedra just chose differently. `[CANON — UESP]` This is explicitly contested within TES lore by multiple in-universe philosophers.

## Lorkhan `[CANON — UESP, but his nature is CONTESTED]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Lorkhan — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Lorkhan  
> **Source:** In-game book: The Monomyth  
> **Source:** In-game book: The Annotated Anuad

- Known by many names: **Shor** (Nords), **Shezarr** (Imperials), **Sep** (Altmer), **Lorkhaj** (Khajiit), **Lorkhan** (scholarly consensus name)
- The **trickster et'Ada** who convinced or tricked the other spirits into creating Mundus. `[CANON — IN-GAME BOOK: The Monomyth]`
- His motivations are deeply `[CONTESTED — by design]`:
  - **Altmer view:** Sep (Lorkhan) is a serpent-trickster who stole the divine light of the et'Ada to make a prison-world; he is the enemy. `[IN-GAME BOOK: The Monomyth, Altmer version]`
  - **Nordic view:** Shor is a heroic figure who gave up everything so mortals could exist; he is a champion of humanity. `[IN-GAME BOOK: The Monomyth, Nordic version]`
  - **Imperial view:** Shezarr is a missing god who sacrificed himself; mourning him is part of the Imperial faith. `[CANON — UESP]`
- His **Heart** was cast away by the other et'Ada as punishment; it fell to Nirn and became the **Stone of the Red Tower**. `[CANON — UESP]`
- Lorkhan is sometimes called the **"dead god"** — his body became the moons (Masser = decaying flesh, Secunda = lesser remnant). `[SOFT CANON — UESP, one in-game book account]`

## The Moons: Masser and Secunda `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Masser — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Masser

- **Masser** (also called **Jode** by Khajiit) — the larger moon; reddish-brown; visibly decaying
- **Secunda** (also called **Jone** by Khajiit) — the smaller moon; pale; pockmarked
- Both are considered the **rotting body of Lorkhan** by some accounts. `[SOFT CANON — one in-game book; not universally accepted]`
- Khajiit physiology is determined by the **phases of both moons** at birth — this is a direct divine function of the moons. `[CANON — IN-GAME BOOK: Words of Clan Mother Ahnissi]`
- The **Lunar Lattice** (Khajiit cosmological concept) holds that the moons maintain the structure of Mundus and the Khajiiti people are its defenders. `[CANON — ESO, UESP]`

## CHIM `[SOFT CANON — debated, in-game books]`
> **Source:** In-game book: Loveletter from the Fifth Era (possibly apocryphal)  
> **Source:** 36 Lessons of Vivec (Vivec's perspective)  
> ⚠️ **CHIM is a complex, contested concept drawn primarily from in-game philosophical texts. Treat all claims about CHIM carefully.**

- CHIM (the word is from the Ehlnofex language; exact translation disputed) describes a state of **perfect self-awareness** — understanding that you are a character in someone else's dream (the Godhead's dream) AND maintaining your individual identity despite that knowledge.
- Failure to maintain identity when realising this leads to **zero-sum** — the person simply ceases to exist (is "erased" from the dream).
- Achieving CHIM means you can **edit reality** — act outside the normal rules of Mundus while remaining within it.
- **Tiber Septim** and **Vivec** are widely believed to have achieved CHIM. `[SOFT CANON — UESP / in-game book inference]`
- ⚠️ CHIM is never explicitly confirmed in any canonical game; it is inferred from obscure in-game texts and developer (Michael Kirkbride) supplementary writings. Treat as `[SOFT CANON]` for mod purposes.

## The Dragon Break `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Dragon Break — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Dragon_Break

- A **Dragon Break** is a period where **linear time shatters** — multiple contradictory timelines exist simultaneously and are all equally true.
- Named for a "break" in Akatosh's (the Dragon of Time's) power over linear causality.
- **Known Dragon Breaks:**
  - **The Middle Dawn (1E 1200–2208):** An 800-year period (Alessian Doctrines era) where time broke due to religious disruption `[CANON — IN-GAME BOOK: The Warp in the West]`
  - **The Warp in the West (3E 417):** The simultaneous resolution of all six ending states of TES II: Daggerfall — caused by multiple activations of the Numidium `[CANON — TES II: Daggerfall; developer confirmation]` All six Daggerfall endings are simultaneously canon.
- ⚠️ Dragon Breaks are used by Bethesda to canonise contradictory game states. `[CANON — developer design intention]`

## The Numidium and Walk-Brass `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** See `06_towers.md` for Tower details  
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Numidium — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Numidium

- The Numidium is simultaneously a **giant brass war-golem**, a **Dwemer theological statement** (a god-machine to replace the gods they rejected), and a **Tower** (Walk-Brass).
- Its activation by Tiber Septim caused a Dragon Break. Its later activation in Daggerfall caused the Warp in the West. `[CANON — UESP]`
- The Numidium "argues with reality" rather than following it — its very existence is an act of rejection of the Mundus as constructed. `[SOFT CANON — UESP philosophical interpretation]`

## The Elder Scrolls (the artifacts themselves) `[CANON — UESP]`
> **Source:** UESP Lore:Elder Scroll — https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Elder_Scroll

- The **Elder Scrolls** are artifacts of immense power that contain fragments of **all possible futures and pasts** simultaneously.
- Reading one without training causes **blindness** (temporary to permanent depending on frequency of reading). `[CANON — TES V: Skyrim]`
- They are neither created nor destroyable by any known means; their origin is unknown even to the gods. `[CANON — UESP]`
- The **Moth Priests** (Imperial cult scholars) are trained to read them; even they go blind eventually. `[CANON — TES IV: Oblivion; TES V: Dawnguard]`
- ⚠️ The Scrolls are a **meta-narrative device** — they represent the unknowability of canon itself; each reading produces different results. `[SOFT CANON — developer design intention]`
